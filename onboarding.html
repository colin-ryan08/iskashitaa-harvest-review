Initial onboarding requirements

The onboarding every new volunteer completes before their first field harvest — a session at the Iskashitaa office and garden — and how they move from there into the tiered role model described in Roles & Expectations.

Before Any Harvest

Onboarding comes first

Onboarding is a required first step for every new volunteer — completed before Hand Picker and before going on any field harvest. No one rides out to a homeowner's site until they've been through it and been cleared by the coordinator. No prior experience is required; onboarding is how a brand-new volunteer becomes ready.

How it works: A new volunteer's first "harvest" is spent at the Iskashitaa office and garden — not in the field. They learn the harvest process, the roles, fruit grading, and where the fruit goes; complete their paperwork; and put the basics into practice with assigned garden tasks. Only once onboarding is complete and the coordinator gives approval do they join a real harvest as a Hand Picker.

The onboarding session, step by step

  1. Start at the office or garden

    The first session happens on-site at Iskashitaa — the office and/or the community garden — rather than at a homeowner's property. It's a calm setting to learn the work before facing the pace of a real harvest.

  2. Learn the harvest process

    Walk through how a harvest runs end to end: the pre-harvest briefing, picking and gathering, weighing and recording, on-site culling, and the site close-out.

  3. Meet the roles and their requirements

    Review the roles across the three tiers and what each one asks of a volunteer — so a newcomer understands what they're starting with and where they could go. See Roles & Expectations.

  4. Train on fruit grading & deterioration

    Learn to tell harvest-quality fruit from first-use, pet-grade, and compost, and how produce deteriorates over time — the same culling criteria applied on every harvest.

  5. Learn how the fruit is used

    See where harvested fruit goes — refugee families, food pantries, and preservation — so the work is connected to its purpose from the very first day.

  6. Complete the paperwork

    Sign the volunteer waiver and finish any remaining volunteer application or intake forms.

  7. Hands-on tasks in the garden

    Time permitting, put the basics into practice with assigned tasks in the Iskashitaa garden — a low-stakes way to build comfort and confidence.

  8. Coordinator approval

    The coordinator confirms the volunteer is ready and clears them for field harvests. This approval is what unlocks the first real harvest as a Hand Picker.

What to Bring

Gear requirements

From the first field harvest onward, the same baseline gear applies across every tier and role — nothing additional is required to take on a new role. (The office-and-garden onboarding session has no special gear requirement.)

Closed-toed shoes

Required for all on-site roles.

Long sleeves

Sun and scratch protection while picking.

Hat & sunscreen

Arizona harvest conditions, most of the year.

Reusable water bottle

No on-site water service.

How It Works

How the tiered role model works

Once the coordinator clears a volunteer through onboarding, they begin their first field harvest as a Hand Picker. From there, roles are organized into three tiers — Foundational, Experienced, and Specialist — rather than a single fixed sequence. Within each tier, roles are listed in a suggested order, but volunteers may try them in any sequence the Harvest Lead feels is appropriate. Moving between tiers is based on demonstrated comfort and readiness, not a fixed harvest count. Returning or experienced volunteers may be placed at an appropriate tier directly by the Harvest Lead.

Three tiers, suggested order

Foundational, Experienced, and Specialist. Order within a tier is a suggestion, not a requirement — the Harvest Lead sequences roles to a volunteer's interest and fit.

Staying is valid

Remaining within a tier long-term is a complete, supported contribution. Experienced and Specialist roles still require demonstrated experience, and a few — like Ladder Picker after Ladder Spotter — carry a non-negotiable prerequisite.

Two separate tracks

Harvest Lead and Driver do not require completion of any tier — they're distinct roles with their own prerequisites. See Roles & Expectations.

Open Items

What onboarding still needs decided

The tiered role model above describes how movement between roles and tiers is meant to work. Four mechanical questions are still open from the June 2026 program review, each with a designated decision-maker.

  • Tracking mechanism — how will each volunteer's current tier and the roles they're cleared for actually be recorded? (leadership / volunteer coordinator)
  • Readiness criteria — should the "2–3 harvests" guidance become a fixed number, or stay a Harvest Lead judgment call? (harvest leads / leadership)
  • Ownership — who maintains the model day-to-day, across harvests and Harvest Leads? (leadership)
  • Onboarding sign-off — confirm how the coordinator's approval to harvest gets recorded (e.g., an onboarding-complete date or cleared-to-harvest flag on the volunteer's Contact, alongside the signed waiver), not just assumed. (leadership / volunteer coordinator)

The Salesforce data model proposed in Salesforce Evaluation & Proposal is built to answer the tracking-mechanism question directly — a volunteer's tier and roles become fields on their Contact record, updated by the Harvest Lead after each harvest.
